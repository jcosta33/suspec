# DX audit — ten-persona cold-adoption simulation (2026-06-12)

Status: complete. Eleven journeys, 64 verified findings, unanimous pilot-middle-slice.

## What this is

A developer-experience audit of Swarm conducted as a simulation: independent personas, each a
plausible member of Swarm's adopter space, arrive at the repo cold, embody the skeptic stance
(refute by default; claims are tested, not believed; every assertion cites file:line), walk a
fixed journey from first contact through real usage to an adopt/no-adopt decision, and score
the experience on an anchored rubric. The simulation was designed before it ran; this section
records the design so the scores can be interpreted — and re-run later for comparison.

## Reliability mechanisms

The four ways a role-played audit can lie, and the countermeasure for each:

1. **Sycophancy / niceness** — personas are instructed that a 3 is not an insult and a 5 is
   rare, that they decide for their own team rather than for Swarm's authors, and that bailing
   mid-journey is valid data. Skepticism is in-character (each card carries a bias grounded in
   the persona's history).
2. **Hallucinated evidence** — every finding requires a repo-relative path, a line number, and
   a verbatim quote; an independent checker agent re-resolves every quote against the repo, and
   findings that fail verification are dropped from the audit (recorded separately as dropped).
3. **Reading instead of doing** — the journey *executes*: each persona literally runs the
   documented adoption (`cp -R` into an isolated /tmp workspace), fills the real templates for
   a feature of their own product, enacts the implementing agent against their own task packet,
   and fills a review packet that must exercise at least one non-Pass path.
4. **Score incomparability** — one fixed 10-dimension rubric with behavioral anchors
   (1 = broken/harmful for me · 2 = major friction, would stop · 3 = workable baseline ·
   4 = clear improvement over my status quo · 5 = exceptional, would advocate), integer scores,
   each score requiring a quoted moment from that persona's own journey as evidence.

Each persona ran in an isolated context with no access to the others' output; convergence
across personas is therefore meaningful (a defect reported independently three times is a
pattern, not an echo).

## The journey protocol (8 stages, in order)

| # | Stage | What happens | Budget |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | arrival | README.md only — does the value proposition land? | ~5 min |
| 2 | orientation | docs/01–03 — the loop, the workspace, where files live | ~15 min |
| 3 | deep-read | the persona's own path through docs + one example walkthrough | ~30–40 min |
| 4 | adoption | execute docs/ADOPTING.md path 1 literally, in /tmp; time it; log every snag | measured |
| 5 | first-artifacts | real intake + spec for scenario task A, using the kit templates | measured |
| 6 | task-and-run | cut a task packet; enact the implementing agent against it; produce the run summary | measured |
| 7 | review-and-close | fill the review packet honestly (≥1 non-Pass path), Close with a finding + board; sketch scenario task B | measured |
| 8 | week-two | project the habits onto the real team; score; verdict | — |

## The rubric (10 dimensions)

first-contact · learnability · time-to-first-loop · conceptual-coherence · workflow-fit ·
template-quality · agent-leverage · review-value · honesty-trust · sustainability — definitions
as given to the personas, plus a gut **overall** (not an average) and an adopt decision from
{adopt-now · pilot-middle-slice · watch · no} with "what would change my mind".

## Results

### Headline

All eleven personas completed the full eight-stage journey — **nobody bailed**, and all eleven
independently reached the same decision: **pilot-middle-slice**. Nobody adopted outright;
nobody walked away. The unanimous middle is itself the audit's clearest result: every persona,
from the BaFin-regulated bank engineer to the solo indie hacker, found the spec → task →
review-packet core worth running on real work *now*, and every one of them refused to bet
their team on the convention-only glue around it (the hand-edited board, the spot-check
discipline, the habits the CLI doesn't yet exist to hold up).

The dimensional profile is sharp and consistent:

- **honesty-trust 4.9/5 — the product's strongest asset.** Ten personas scored it 5 and went
  looking for the lie first ("I spent three hours failing to catch it lying" — Rafael). The
  honesty legend, the labeled conventions, the hedged citations, and the self-rejecting
  bibliography repeatedly converted skeptics' hostility into trust.
- **review-value 4.2, template-quality 4.1 — the wedge holds under real use.** Filled (not
  read), the templates forced out real ambiguities pre-code (Elena's chair-pooling, Marco's
  Treasury limit) and the packet caught classes of failure each persona recognized from their
  own postmortems (a zero-test green run, a sqlite lock no-op, a missing valid-path AC).
- **sustainability 3.0 — unanimous, and the floor.** Eleven out of eleven scored it exactly 3,
  each independently predicting the same death order: the status board dies first, then the
  spot-check discipline, while packets/specs/findings survive because they have an economic
  reason to. The framework's own honesty ("nothing enforces this") names the disease but ships
  no cure until swarm-cli.
- **workflow-fit 3.5 — the cost of the unmet shapes.** Every score below 4 traces to a
  documented persona shape the docs never address: multi-workspace agencies, one-spec
  two-platform mobile, HIL evidence, stochastic evals, drive-by OSS PRs, sandboxed enterprise
  agents.

### What the panel converged on — defects

Ranked by independent reports × severity. Each was found without knowledge of the others'
journeys.

1. **The run summary is homeless (9 of 11 personas; 7 rated it MAJOR).** Every surface demands
   it (task template instruction 5, implement-task rule 8, docs/02, docs/07) and docs/08 calls
   it the raw material for the Evidence column — but no template section, file convention, or
   location exists. The only stated location is "the PR description", which does not exist at
   handoff time. Every persona who reached stage 6 had to invent a home. This is the single
   highest-confidence finding the audit produced.
2. **The evidence model assumes CLI-runnable checks (4 personas, 4 MAJOR).** Ingrid (HIL rigs:
   the user-tier Pass rule admits only pasted output or a CI link, while the framework's own
   `manual` verification method and SOL-V010's named-human rule live in reference pages the
   user tier never surfaces); Marco (sandboxed enterprise agents that *cannot* execute);
   Tomás (flaky suites: one green paste is Pass-grade evidence, nondeterminism unaddressed at
   user tier); Yuki (stochastic outputs: same code, different numbers — and the only
   baseline-protocol guide scopes itself to systems metrics, excluding model quality).
3. **No retrofit path when work arrives without upstream artifacts (3 personas, 2 MAJOR).**
   The packet presumes a task with scoped IDs. Priya's external PR, Rafael's drive-by 1,400-line
   stranger PR, and Dex's week-one bug with no covering spec all required inventing the same
   undocumented post-hoc flow (snapshot the work as intake → spec as acceptance bar → reviewer
   produces all evidence). Rafael proved it works; nobody should have to invent it.
4. **The Commands table doesn't survive real estates (4 personas, 1 MAJOR).** One singular slot
   per command kind fails polyglot monorepos (Amara), multi-repo workspaces (Tomás, Ingrid),
   and ships only the four app-shaped slots of the nine-slot contract (Viktor — cmdSecurity
   missing for the persona who needs it most).
5. **"Two minutes" measured at 10–16 minutes by five independent stopwatches.** The copy is
   seconds; the mandatory fill steps are not. The worst oversell in an otherwise scrupulously
   honest repo, flagged by five personas precisely because the honesty elsewhere set the bar.
6. **The deep library is buried (2 MAJOR).** docs/library/code-skills — fix-flaky-test,
   write-performance, write-testing, the framework's best answers to two personas' core
   problems — is reachable only from a reference-page index; Tomás and Yuki both failed to
   find it from their problem until late.
7. **Team-shape gaps, each owned by one persona but structural:** no team adoption path — roles,
   who writes specs, who reviews (Amara, MAJOR); one-spec-N-platforms scope-sharing is
   forbidden by split-work's coverage rule (Tomás, MAJOR); review-status enum has no terminal
   value for waive-and-merge, the most common honest exception outcome (Dex, MAJOR; Waived
   exists only in the advanced tier per Viktor's merge-gate finding); kit reviewer guide sets a
   stricter evidence bar than docs/08 (Priya, MAJOR); Windows symlink clones fail silently
   (Marco, MAJOR); checks.yaml's machine-shadow claim has gaps and a REFLEXION/EVIBOUND
   citation drift in checks/README.md (Viktor).
8. **Board weaknesses (4 personas):** task states lack `blocked` (Yuki, Elena); the shipped
   root `status.md` contains `{{slug}}` template residue (Amara); no cross-workspace
   aggregation for portfolio teams (Elena).

### What the panel converged on — strengths

- The README's anti-promise block and the honesty legend repeatedly *bought* the second look
  from personas primed to leave ("read like someone who has been burned by frameworks
  before" — Elena; "no framework README I've survived has done that" — Marco).
- Adoption mechanics work as documented: `cp -R` preserved symlinks on every run; Claude Code
  discovered the guides with zero wiring; measured copy-to-first-spec ranged 13–50 minutes.
- The templates earn their keep **by being filled**: Open questions and Dropped from sources
  did real work in ten of eleven journeys; the blocking-question rule correctly held two
  specs at draft.
- The review packet caught genuinely missable defects in enacted runs: a green test run that
  executed zero tests (Rafael), `lockForUpdate()` as a sqlite no-op (Elena), a spec with no
  valid-path AC (Marco — the packet caught the *spec's* bug), an IAM policy with plausible
  wrong scoping (Viktor).
- The artifacts double as documents personas already owe someone: auditor-readable evidence
  (Marco — "I would show one to BaFin"), client proof-of-work (Elena), onboarding context
  (Elena's rotation test: 15 minutes to transfer project state "better than our current
  Slack-archaeology").

### Methodological limitations (read before acting on scores)

1. **Score uniformity is partly an artifact.** All eleven personas share one base model;
   despite isolated contexts, anchored rubrics, and distinct biases, the numeric scores
   converged far more than a human panel would (every overall exactly 4; every sustainability
   exactly 3). Treat the **relative** dimensional ordering and the **findings** as the signal;
   treat absolute calibration and inter-rater agreement as unmeasured.
2. **The products are fictions.** Codebases were enacted, not real; agent runs were sketched
   where toolchains were absent (several personas turned this into genuine evidence-rule
   tests — exit-127s pasted honestly — but no finding here proves the loop at production
   scale or over weeks).
3. **Week-two projections are projections.** Sustainability 3.0 is a unanimous *prediction*,
   not an observation. The pre-registered pilot (spec-first vs prompt-only) remains the only
   way to convert it into data.
4. **Verification covers citation accuracy, not judgment.** The checker confirmed every quote
   resolves and every issue is a fair reading; whether a MAJOR is truly major remains the
   persona's call.
